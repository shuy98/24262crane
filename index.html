About this project

The objective of this project is to design and build a mechanism that is powered by a standard servomotor, which will lift a cylindrical weight. The mechanism is to be clamped in a given position while the other end lifts a weight that slides along a vertical post. Between the clamping area for the base of the design and the post holding the weight sits an obstacle. The mechanism may not touch the obstacle or any other surface besides the base and the sliding weight. We will build such a mechanism that is no more than 20Oz, and it should be able to lift a 1lb weight at least 2in high.


Our Mechanism

Features

We constructed an accurate model of our design using SolidWorks and used Finite Element Analysis to study the stress distribution on the crane, in order to save material from places where the stress is low and add material wherever necessary without actually physically building the crane.

We used SolidWorks assemblies to simulate the lifting process to help us adjust the position of the servo mount. We also employed a 3-point fixture method to make sure the servo is securely attached to the main frame and is able to withstand the moments and forces during a lift.

We employed a three-part lifting arm mechanism (as shown in the picture above) to achieve optimal angle of contact between the arm and the weight and to maximize lifting distance. We also added a protection cap at the tip of our servo arm where it makes contact with the underside of the weight in order to reduce friction and prevent the servo arm from sliding out during operation.

We designed our counterweight arm to provide just enough moment to assist the servo in lifting the weight, but not to negatively affect the lifting capacity by (a) adjusting the length so it doesn’t make contact with the ground, and (b) using a guiding rail on the side of the main frame to prevent collision between the counterweight and the main body.

Performance Analysis

Actual Distance Lifted: 4.78 inches


Theoretical Servo Torque Calculation

Assumptions in calculation: the load exerts a force of 1 lb. perpendicular to the servo arm; the gravitational force of the counter weight always acts perpendicular to the counterweight arm. Servo has torque 57oz-in.

Distance between servo rotating axis and the contact point of the servo arm is 3 inches.
Distance between servo rotating axis and the CM of counterweight is 8.5 inches.
Total torque exerted by the servo is approximately:

16oz · 3in – 4.7oz · 8.5in = 8.05oz-in

which is,

100% · (8.05oz-in⁄57oz-in) = 14.12%

of total torque.

Theoretical Lift Distance Calculation

Assumptions: the point of contact between the crane and the weight is fixed, and the crane does not move during the process.

The angular rotation of the servo arm is approximately 90 degrees; the servo arm rests initially at a 45-degree angle with the table surface. Let initial height be 0, we have final height z:

z = (3in · sin(90⁄2)) · 2 = 4.24in

Structural Principles

In this part of the main frame, the internal loading is mostly bending moment in the vertical plane. Therefore, we decided to use four L-shaped 0.75’’ aluminum strips on the top and bottom of the frame, with a larger spacing in the vertical direction than in the horizontal, in order to increase the second moment of inertia (I) and make it more resistance to bending in the vertical plane. We also added L-shaped trusses in between the bottom and top plane to limit deflection.

In this part of the main frame, our major concern of internal loading comes from torsion (as shown below). To limit deflection, we added two flat aluminum strips oriented in opposite diagonals on the top and bottom respectively. This configuration increases Ip near the base and therefore is more resistant to torsion. We can also adjust the tension in these strips to control the angle of the main frame under loads.
